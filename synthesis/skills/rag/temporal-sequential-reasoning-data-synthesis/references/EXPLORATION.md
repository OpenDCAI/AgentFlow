# Phase 1: Environment Exploration Guide
* **Exploration Strategy**:
  - **Harvest explicit time anchors.** During exploration, gather documents that each contribute a date, year, quarter, release order, or citation-time relation relevant to the target question. Normalize these anchors into one comparable representation before assessing difficulty. Temporal tasks become unstable if dates remain in heterogeneous raw forms.
  - **Mix absolute and relative temporal cues.** Use a blend of explicit dates, named periods, and relative phrases like 'earlier', 'later', or 'after sixteen years'. Then verify that the trajectory can convert them into a single ordered frame. This helps the skill generalize beyond date-string matching.
  - **Probe trend and ordering variants.** Explore both event-order questions and value-over-time questions so the capability is not reduced to one narrow pattern. Keep cases that require comparison across multiple time points, not just one date lookup. A good temporal suite should include before/after, trend, and gap questions.
  - **Check for misleading local recency cues.** Intentionally create variants where the latest document is not the decisive one or where a recent-looking passage is irrelevant to the asked interval. This prevents the model from using recency as a shortcut for temporal correctness. An important edge case is a trend question where the middle year is the turning point.
* **Target Trajectory Profile**:
  - **Temporal normalization first.** A good trajectory converts all time references into a common comparable frame before answering. That may mean mapping named releases to dates, quarters to calendar sequence, or offsets to concrete years. Skipping normalization is the main source of chronological mistakes.
  - **Per-timepoint evidence capture.** The trace should preserve which document supports each specific timepoint or interval. This is especially important for trends and multi-year summaries. Without per-timepoint capture, later ordering errors are hard to debug.
  - **Ordering or trend computation is explicit.** The trajectory must show the ordering or change analysis step rather than jumping from raw dates or numbers straight to a verdict. Use a short intermediate representation such as sorted events or ordered values. This explicitness is what makes the reasoning reusable.
  - **Answer wording matches temporal granularity.** The final answer should reflect the exact temporal relation supported by the evidence, such as before/after, increased then decreased, or a precise derived year. Do not broaden a punctual relation into a vague summary. Granularity discipline prevents hidden semantic drift.
